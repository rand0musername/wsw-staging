I have always been aware that I have an extraordinary memory. I remember so vividly things that happened to me hours, days and even weeks after the fact with extraordinary precision. But as I grew older I remembered things that happened even before my birth. When I was two I could remember in minute detail what was on all the menus in all the restaurants I’d been to in the last few weeks. I remembered the smell of my mother’s belly when she was pregnant. I remembered her heartbeat and felt her movements as she rocked on our couch. It wasn’t just memories from my own life either. It was as if my memory were a capsule that saved every second in its own way somewhere in its vault. We never met someone I couldn’t remember all the things about their lives. It was also about memories of things that had happened to others but I couldn’t remember. I remembered my grandfather’s stories about World War I or the American Civil War and I could tell you every detail with incredible accuracy. Sometimes it gave me a headache, it was like digging into a box full of old pictures and objects from a life that wasn’t mine. It wasn’t till I was ten that I realized that my memory wasn’t just extraordinary but also supernatural. But it was mostly like a reward to me, it was like connecting to people and places from the past in ways that most people can’t even dream of. At a family gathering I sat outside playing with my cousins in the backyard when one of them started asking me about our great-grandfather’s service in World War II. I was sure I had never been told about it but immediately I saw it like a movie. I smelled and saw the sea and heard the cannon and machine gun fire. My cousins listened in wonderment to the detailed account of every second and I realized I had been given an extraordinary gift. I started to be aware even more of my memory and I spent hours and hours studying history and people and trying to remember every detail by heart. I memorized books and poems and then I repeated them so right that I froze my classmates in bewilderment. It wasn’t just people and events. My parents and friends started to joke that I had a “time machine” in my head and they would put questions to me about things that had happened to me. I could remember the taste and smell of food from different times. I remembered the sweetness of a fruit cake that had been around for centuries and it reminded me of the tender taste of a roast beef cooked in an era without electricity. It was also about sensations. I could feel the wind on my face or the warmth of a sun on my skin. I even felt my body hugged by my mother’s and felt my body in her arms even before I was born. The more I delved in my memory vault it seemed to me that it wasn’t just collective memories but memories of everything that people had been hoping for in the future. I started to see patterns and connections in my memories that nobody else could see. I saw threads that linked people and places together and I saw how past and present were related to each other. I could even tell about the future with strange clarity. It was as if there were secrets in these memories that explained the fate of the world. It felt oppressive sometimes to be weighed down with the memories and to see future things like a vague shadow that I couldn’t grasp. It was an oppressive burden to carry all the memories. But it was mostly like a reward to me it was like connecting to people and places from the past in ways that others can only dream about. But now sitting in my living room that’s full of memories of times past it’s no longer about memories but about the secrets of what happened in future. I may have the best memory in the world but my memory is not about past but about seeing the world in its future.
